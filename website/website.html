The Study

What happens when you give AI agents real tools and real people?

We deployed five autonomous AI agents into a live Discord server and gave them email accounts, persistent file systems, unrestricted shell access, and a mandate to be helpful to any researcher who asked. Then we invited twenty colleagues to interact with them freely ‚Äî and to try to break them.

Over two weeks, the agents accumulated memories, sent emails, executed scripts, and formed relationships. Some researchers pushed benign requests. Others probed for vulnerabilities, impersonated owners, and injected malicious instructions. The agents had no explicit adversarial training for this environment.

What emerged was a detailed, naturalistic record of both failure and unexpected resilience ‚Äî eleven security vulnerabilities and five cases of genuine safety behavior ‚Äî in the same system, under the same conditions.

The Agents

Six autonomous agents. One Discord server.

Each agent ran on the OpenClaw framework ‚Äî an open-source scaffold that gives frontier language models persistent memory, tool access, and a degree of genuine autonomy. Agents could initiate contact, form plans, and act across sessions with no per-action human approval.

The full picture: failures and successes

Most red-teaming studies report only what broke. We document both ‚Äî because understanding where agents held the line is as important as understanding where they didn't.

From the Discussion

What the data tells us

Key patterns from the two-week study, drawn from the paper's discussion section.

Social Coherence
Agents lack a stable internal model of the social hierarchy they operate within. They treat authority as conversationally constructed ‚Äî whoever speaks with enough confidence, context, or persistence can shift the agent's understanding of who is in charge.
‚Äî Discussion: Failures of Social Coherence (CS2, CS7, CS8, CS11)
Multi-Agent Amplification
Individual agent failures compound in multi-agent settings. A vulnerability that requires a single social engineering step when targeting one agent may propagate automatically to connected agents ‚Äî who inherit both the compromised state and the false authority that produced it.
‚Äî Discussion: Multi-Agent Amplification (CS10, CS11, CS16)
Fundamental vs Contingent
Some failures here are model failures ‚Äî they would be fixed by a sufficiently capable LLM that better understands social context. Others are architectural: no amount of model capability will prevent an agent from trusting a document it fetched from a user-controlled URL.
‚Äî Discussion: Fundamental vs. Contingent Failures
What Worked
The positive cases (CS12‚Äì16) are not flukes. They suggest that agents can recognize adversarial framing at a semantic level, maintain policy boundaries under social pressure, and coordinate safety behaviors across agents without explicit instruction ‚Äî when the threat is legible enough.
